Chapter 1

  • Said by her father to have “something more of quickness than her sisters.”

Chapter 3

  • Mostly unaffected by what Mr. Darcy said about her since she just joked about it.
  • “lively, playful disposition, which delighted in everything ridiculous.”

Chapter 4

  • Thinks Mr. Bingley has a complete character.
  • Isn't convinced by how Jane describes the Bingley sisters since she doesn't really like them.
  • Has more "quickness of observation and less pliancy of temper than her sister."
  • "with a judgement too unassailed by any attention to herself, she was very little disposed to approve them."

Chapter 5

  • Promises her mother that she'd never dance with Mr. Darcy.
  • "I could easily forgive his pride, if he had not mortified mine."
  • Continues to hold the "tolerable" comment against him.

Chapter 6

  • Couldn't like the Bingley sisters since they were conceited towards everyone, even her sister.
  • Feels like its obvious that Jane likes Mr. Bingley back and that he'd be dumb to not find that out.
  • Turns down Mr. Darcy when he asks to dance with her.

Chapter 7

  • Was worried about her sister being sick so she showed up at Netherfield by walking there despite what her mother said or what the Bingleys would think of her.
  • Was extremely concerned about Jane and spent most of her time with her by her bedside.

Chapter 8

  • Went back to disliking the Bingley sisters after they seemed to be indifferent towards Jane when she wasn't in front of them.
  • "Her manners were pronounced to be very bad indeed, a mixture of pride and impertinence; she had no conversation, no style, no beauty," the Bingley sisters' thoughts on Elizabeth.
  • Would not leave Jane's side at all.
  • "I deserve neither praise nor such censure... I am not a great reader, and I have pleasure in many things."
  • Challenges Mr. Darcy and Miss Bingley's definition of accomplished women and claims that no woman fits that description since she felt that they were too demanding.
  • "Elizabeth Bennet... is one of those young ladies who seek to recommend themselves to the other sex, by undervaluing their own," as said by Miss Bingley.
